Introduction

There are reams of literature on what makes a good cryptographic hash. Every ten years or so we have a competition to pick some new ones. You may have seen pseudocode. You may have seen diagrams of the inner workings. Maybe you can even read and understand the code. But have you ever seen a good illustration of how they work? I don't think I have.

Recently I was toying around with Bitcoin. I understood pretty quickly why CPU and GPU miners were no longer competetive with custom hardware, but what I couldn't figure out is why GPU miners weren't much faster than CPU miners (at least not on my hardware).

I wanted visuals, something a bit more accessible, like mining bitcoin with pencil and paper, but with less talking and more pictures. What I'd like to show, among other things, is how a small change to the input changes the output drastically.

If you need a refresher in hashes, I've made a modest attempt

The Plan

Okay, this all sounds pretty good, but SHA-2 hashes are pretty big, and for some versions the internal data is twice that big. This is a SHA-256 hash:

We're not trying to invent a new graphical representation of hashes here. At any rate, if I put two of these next to each other you'd be hard pressed to tell them apart. This also isn't bar codes for cryptographers. Instead, we're trying to visualize the hashing lifecycle. For our purposes, instead of using these colored bars to represent a hash, we're going to use them to represent the difference between two hashes.

In other words, a colored bar means the bit changed.
